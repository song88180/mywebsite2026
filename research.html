Research

My future work asks how evolution operates across natural populations, human complex traits, reproductive systems, and artificial worlds built for open-ended evolution.

Elucidating Molecular Evolution in Real Natural Environments

Natural environments fluctuate across time, space, species interactions, and gene flow, yet much of molecular evolution is still inferred from simplified laboratory systems or historical genomic patterns. I aim to measure evolutionary dynamics directly in near-natural and natural populations by combining controllable ecological experiments, high-throughput genome editing, lineage barcoding, deep mutational scanning, long-term field sampling, and ultra-deep targeted sequencing. This work will quantify the relative abundance and effects of beneficial, neutral, and deleterious mutations, test how antagonistic pleiotropy operates outside the laboratory, and estimate how selection, drift, migration, balancing selection, and environmental structure jointly shape allele-frequency change. The broader goal is to reveal the real-time molecular dynamics of adaptation and drift in nature.

Evolution and Genetic Architecture of Human Complex Traits

Large biobanks now make it possible to study how complex traits evolve in rapidly changing societies, but most discoveries still come from European-ancestry cohorts. I plan to integrate East Asian resources such as the China Kadoorie Biobank, BioBank Japan, and Taiwan Biobank with global datasets to compare the genetic architecture of complex traits across populations. Key questions include how transferable GWAS signals and polygenic scores are across ancestries, how substructure within Chinese populations affects trait evolution, and how social changes such as education, contraception, disease burden, and fertility decline reshape selection pressures. Using GWAS, heritability, genetic correlation, selection-scan, polygenic-score, ancient-DNA, and structural-equation approaches, this direction will connect molecular mechanisms of complex traits with evolutionary medicine and population change.

Evolution of Sex, Reproduction, and Inheritance Systems

Sex is central to biological evolution because it reshuffles genetic variation, drives sexual selection, and creates extraordinary diversity in reproduction and inheritance. I plan to investigate several connected questions: how offspring sex ratio evolves and is mechanistically controlled, how sexual and asexual reproduction are maintained or replaced, how same-sex sexual behavior evolves in natural populations, how sex chromosomes originate and change, and how non-canonical inheritance systems influence adaptation. This direction will combine population genetics, comparative genomics, field and experimental systems, and large-scale phenotype-genotype data to link genes, reproductive strategies, behavior, and fitness. The goal is to build an integrated empirical and theoretical framework for understanding the evolution of sex-related traits.

Digital Organisms and Artificial Life

I also aim to build virtual evolutionary ecosystems in which embodied digital organisms evolve morphology, behavior, and neural control through open-ended selection. Instead of designing intelligence top-down, this platform will let agents compete, reproduce, mutate, interact with resources, and coevolve in physics-based worlds. The project will begin with scalable 2D simulations and expand toward richer 3D environments, using generative genotype-phenotype mappings, neural controllers, resource systems, quality-diversity methods, and eventually recombination and mating-type systems. This work will explore whether cognition, modularity, self-assembly, cooperation, communication, and sexual reproduction can emerge from virtual evolution. It may also offer a new route toward adaptive, energy-efficient AI and a powerful simulation tool for evolutionary biology.
