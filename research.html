Research

Elucidating Molecular Evolution in Real Natural Environments

Although the theoretical foundations of population genetics were well established in the early twentieth century, the real-world dynamics of molecular evolution in nature remain poorly understood even today.

I aim to track evolutionary dynamics directly in near-natural and natural populations by combining high-throughput genome editing, lineage barcoding, deep mutational scanning, long-term field sampling, and ultra-deep targeted sequencing. This work will quantify the relative abundance and effects of beneficial, neutral, and deleterious mutations, and reveal how selection, drift, and migration jointly shape the molecular evolution.

Evolution and Genetic Architecture of Human Complex Traits

Large biobanks now make it possible to study how human complex traits evolve in rapidly changing societies, but most discoveries still come from European-ancestry cohorts.

I plan to integrate East Asian biobank resources with global datasets to compare the genetic architecture of complex traits across populations. Key questions include (1) how transferable GWAS signals and polygenic scores are across ancestries, (2) how genetic architecture and phenotypic selections vary across the population substructure of China, and (3) how social changes such as education, contraception, health care, and domestic transition reshape selection pressures.

Using GWAS, heritability, genetic correlation, polygenic-score, ancient DNA, and structural equation approaches, this direction will connect molecular mechanisms of complex traits with evolutionary medicine and population change.

Evolution of Sex, Reproduction, and Inheritance Systems

Sex is central to evolution and creates extraordinary diversity in reproduction and inheritance.

I plan to investigate several connected questions: (1) how offspring sex ratio evolves and is mechanistically controlled, (2) how sexual and asexual reproduction are maintained or replaced, (3) how same-sex sexual behavior evolves in natural populations, (4) how sex chromosomes originate and change, and (5) the diversity of non-canonical inheritance systems.

This direction will combine population genetics, comparative genomics, field sample collections, and large-scale phenotype-genotype data to link genes, sex chromosomes, reproductive strategies, behavior, and fitness.

Digital Organisms and Artificial Life

Inspired by Karl Sims's early work, I aim to build virtual evolutionary ecosystems in which embodied digital organisms evolve morphology, behavior, and neural control through open-ended selection. Instead of designing intelligence top-down, this platform will let agents compete, reproduce, mutate, interact with resources, and coevolve in physics-based worlds. The project will explore generative genotype-phenotype mappings, neural controllers, resource systems, quality-diversity methods, and eventually recombination and mating-type systems.

This work will investigate whether cognition, modularity, self-assembly, cooperation, communication, and sexual reproduction can emerge from virtual evolution. It may also offer a new route toward adaptive, energy-efficient AI and a powerful simulation tool for evolutionary biology.
